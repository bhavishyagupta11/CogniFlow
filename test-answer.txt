BERT, which stands for Bidirectional Encoder Representations from Transformers, is a language representation model designed to pre-train deep bidirectional representations from unlabeled text [1]. It achieves this by jointly conditioning on both left and right contexts across all its layers [1].

The architecture of the BERT model is a multi-layer bidirectional Transformer encoder [2]. Two sizes have been released: BERT-base, with 12 layers, 768 hidden units, 12 attention heads, and 110 million parameters, and BERT-large, with 24 layers, 1024 hidden units, 16 attention heads, and 340 million parameters [2]. Its input representation combines token embeddings, segmentation embeddings, and position embeddings, utilizing WordPiece embeddings with a 30,000-token vocabulary [2, 3].

BERT is pre-trained using tasks such as a binarized next sentence prediction task [2]. Fine-tuning BERT for various downstream tasks is straightforward due to the Transformer's self-attention mechanism, which allows for minimal changes; task-specific inputs and outputs are plugged into BERT, and all parameters are fine-tuned end-to-end [3]. BERT has achieved state-of-the-art results on eleven NLP tasks, including GLUE, SQuAD v1.1, and SQuAD v2.0, with pre-training tasks contributing to its effectiveness [3]. BERT models are also used in components like the Dense Passage Retriever (DPR) to encode queries and documents into dense vectors for retrieval in other systems [4].
